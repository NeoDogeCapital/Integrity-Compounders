## The Business

NVIDIA designs and manufactures graphics processing units (GPUs), system-on-chip units (SoCs), and related software platforms. The company operates across three primary segments: Data Center (AI training and inference accelerators, networking), Gaming (GeForce GPUs for enthusiasts and casual gamers), and Professional Visualization (Quadro GPUs for design and content creation). NVIDIA's economic engine centers on selling high-margin proprietary silicon and increasingly, software platforms like CUDA that create ecosystem lock-in.

The competitive advantage rests on three pillars: a decade-long head start in parallel computing architecture, the CUDA software moat that makes switching costs prohibitive for developers, and a scale advantage in AI infrastructure that compounds with each training cycle. However, competitive threats are mounting from hyperscalers developing custom silicon (Google TPUs, Amazon Trainium, Microsoft Maia), AMD's aggressive MI300 roadmap, and potential commoditization as AI architectures mature. The 107% ROIC and 64% operating margin reflect genuine moat strength, but sustainability of these metrics is the core debate.
